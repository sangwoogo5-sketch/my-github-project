MARGARET BOONE RAPPAPORT, PhD
Co-Founder
The Human Sentience Project LLC
PURCELLVILLE, VA
Dr. Margaret Boone Rappaport, a sought-after expert in space science, artificial intelligence (AI) and ecotheology, is pioneering a novel approach to human habitation on the moon. In "The Human Factor in the Settlement of the Moon: An Interdisciplinary Approach," published by Springer Publishing, she and fellow scholars discuss the challenges and feats required to inhabit lunar settlements and the subsequent benefits for individuals living and working in lunar habitations. Harmonizing the hard sciences with sociopolitical, legal, medical, religious and philosophical frameworks, among others, and leveraging her rich background in human cognitive evolution and anthropology, Dr. Rappaport is uniquely positioned to advance and raise awareness of these future challenges.

Notably, "The Human Factor in the Settlement of the Moon" is composed of several distinct sections, each integrating various disciplines to illustrate the central aspects of society that make humankind whole. Introducing the book with engineering, industrial and agricultural perspectives, it progresses to include chapters on the medical components of lunar habitation, as well as various societal, political and legal outlooks. Finally, the book details religious, philosophical and social perspectives, enabling readers of diverse backgrounds to consider an interdisciplinary approach to what has traditionally been a highly specialized industry.

Beyond her publishing work, Dr. Rappaport has served as the co-founder of The Human Sentience Project LLC since 2014, where she applies her expertise to conduct extensive research and promote an in-depth understanding of topics such as space science, ecotheology and AI across the world. Having published six other books since the inception of her career – in addition to several widely-cited articles in scientific journals – Dr. Rappaport also boasts an impressive background in biology and cultural anthropology. To this end, she previously served as a contractual consultant in Washington, DC, from 1975 to 2005, specializing in minority health. Furthermore, she was the president of Policy Research Methods Inc. from 1986 to 2006 and a professor of anthropology and sociology at Georgetown University, The George Washington University and Prince George's Community College.

Other publications to Dr. Rappaport's credit also include the highly cited article, "Social and Cultural Factors in the Etiology of Low Birthweight among Disadvantaged Blacks" in 1985 and the book "Capital Crime: Black Infant Mortality in America" in 1989. She also co-authored the books "Space Science and Astronomy Theatre" and "The Emergence of Religion in Human Evolution" in 2017 and 2021, respectively. Likewise, Dr. Rappaport has authored works on ecotheology, a theological discipline that examines the relationship between nature and religion and how global religious practices influence environmental conservation.

Dr. Rappaport holds a Bachelor of Arts in biology from The George Washington University, a Master of Education in Social Foundations of Education from the University of Florida, and a Master of Arts in anthropology and a Doctor of Philosophy in cultural anthropology from The Ohio State University. Testifying to her longstanding success, she was honored with a Praxis Award from the Washington Association of Professional Anthropologists.

Qatar, which accounts for about 20% of global supply, shut down its main LNG plant on Monday after it was attacked by drones from Iran. Even if Qatar's plant were back up and running, there's no way to get energy products out of the Persian Gulf. The Strait of Hormuz, which takes products from the Gulf to the Arabian Sea and then mostly to Asia, is effectively blocked. 

The price of LNG in Europe shot up 67% on the week, the most since Russia invaded Ukraine in 2022. The disruption has been a boon to U.S. exporters of LNG. The combination of higher oil prices and a weak job market hammered stocks and bonds and lifted the Cboe Volatility Index, or VIX, to its highest levels in nearly a year. OPINION
CHINA-U.S. Paddles Over Politics
By William Jones
This year sees the 55th anniversary of the famous Ping-Pong Diplomacy, a historical turning point that began to thaw the frozen relationship between the United States and the People's Republic of China that began at the latter's founding in 1949. A chance meeting between a pair of individuals from each country's ping-pong teams helped reduce tension between the nations, both of which were beginning to understand that the "wall of separation" between the two was negatively impacting both. Although President Richard Nixon's administration at the time was intent on rebuilding the U.S. relationship with China, the prevailing atmosphere of Cold War hysteria remained a powerful obstacle. Convincing all stakeholders to embrace such a radical shift in foreign policy was no easy feat. 

People-to-people power
Today, ideological tensions still run high in China-U.S. relations. But added to that is a new layer: rivalry. China has emerged as a major force in technology, making it something of a genuine competitor to the U.S. Meanwhile, the desire of President Donald Trump's administration to return to an era of unchallenged American military and technological dominance has created what can feel like an unbridgeable gap between the two countries. Yet there is a powerful counterweight: the genuine need for stable trade and investment ties. Both nations require a functional economic relationship to prosper. The Trump administration's recent move to ease some of its punitive measures on Chinese goods suggests an acknowledgment of this reality: If the U.S. economy is to thrive, some degree of cooperation is not optional. But given the restrictive actions against China imposed by the U.S. in recent years, it may take some time to create the necessary climate where this "rivalry" can be transformed into constructive competition. A report by the European Central Bank from March 2026 found that "companies that make significant use of AI are about 4 percent more likely to take on additional staff". US research firm Gartner predicts that by 2027, half of companies that reduced headcount citing AI will rehire employees into similar roles. So what, precisely, do we know? We know that we do not know what artificial intelligence will look like in five years, let alone twenty. Much of today's certainty is, in reality, projection—often from those with significant stakes in selling AI models, platforms or infrastructure. Predictions may attract capital and headlines. But leadership cannot rest on prediction. Leadership rests on judgment. While the applications of technology evolve at extraordinary speed, the fundamentals remain constant. The laws of gravity do not change. The scientific method does not change. Economic incentives, human behaviour, and organisational dynamics still shape outcomes. Skills have always been disrupted. We once worked without Word, without Excel, without mobile phones. Skills evolved then, and they will evolve again. That is not unprecedented; it is the pattern of history. What is unprecedented is the speed. We are living through a global transformation defined less by novelty than by acceleration. The turns are faster and sharper. Entire business models can be challenged within months. Four structural shifts underpin this moment: pervasive digital transformation; devices that connect us everywhere; the massive production of data; and the global accessibility of that data through cloud infrastructure. 

The strategic question for executives is not whether AI will change business. It is whether leaders possess the intellectual tools to interrogate its limits, assess its risks, and integrate it responsibly. This requires more than technical fluency. It requires scientific literacy: the ability to understand models and their assumptions. It requires geopolitical awareness: the recognition that technological systems operate within regulatory frameworks, power structures, and competing national strategies. And it requires managerial competence: the capacity to align people, incentives, and institutions around sustainable transformation. In moments of rapid acceleration, the most radical act may be to slow down. Leaders must create space to think critically, to challenge the headlines, and to examine consequences. They must step out of operational urgency and into intellectual reflection. Not to resist innovation, but to understand it deeply enough to govern it wisely. Executive education, at its best, offers precisely this: structured time to step back, analyse, debate, and recalibrate alongside peers confronting similar uncertainties. It is not about chasing every new tool. It is about strengthening the foundations so that when the next technological wave arrives, leaders don't react. Instead, they respond with discernment. The future of AI remains uncertain. The institutions and companies that will thrive are not those that predict the future most loudly, but those that cultivate the judgment to navigate it responsibly. In the age of artificial intelligence, judgment is strategy.
